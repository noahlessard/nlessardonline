# Pi and my opinions on AI  
### Github Link: <Link href="https://github.com/noahlessard/.pi">here</Link>

It's been about 4 years since LLMs passed the turing test and became generally useful, and ever since no one has been able to turn away from talking about them. People familiar with software are expected even more than usual to have opinions on it, so I think it's about time I document my thoughts about the subject. Not that I am an expert or anything, just another perspective and perhaps some guidance. I suppose its also worth noting I wrote this in the summer of 2026, and I focused on the current state of AI, not anything past or future. No lamenting the good old days or panicking about future super-intelligence. I also mostly wrote this about using AI for software and learning, other subjects are only briefly touched on. 

This post will be roughly split up into two sections: the first will be posted here and will mostly be about subjective opinions on how best to use AI. The second will be about more practical, technical topics and what tools I see value in. If you don't care about the softer side of things, skip this blog and check out the Github link at the top of the page. It contains my local AI setup, agent files, skills and sandboxes. It also has my opinons on other grounded matters like cost, security, and environmental impact.

My core thesis I've developed over the last 4 years being around AI is that I think it is helpful to think about it in terms of a specific simile, since it will hit upon many underlying truths about and consequences of the technology. After all, we usually learn about something new by comparing it to something old. My best simile is that using AI is like using a really powerful google search. It is such a powerful way of "looking up" information that it doesn't feel like looking things up anymore, its more like asking the librarian yourself. 

To start with a simple, if sightly off topic example, consider AI being used for "generative artwork." In my opinion, the "looking up" metaphor works here, because while it is true you can look up almost any piece of artwork you want, it's silly to imagine that you "made" that artwork. It is also true that commissioning an artist or drawing something yourself will almost always be a better solution than trying to google the exact image you are looking for. By applying the simile here, I hope I've made the foreign idea of "AI art" seem less alien, and more of something that has been happening for a long time: people just googling and reposting images, claiming them as their own. 

To extend this point to something more utilized, I do believe that asking an AI a coding question, or to have it write code for you, is pretty similar to the olden days of looking up things on stackoverflow or google. Is it yours? No, not if you just copy and paste from stackoverflow. But if you copy a piece of code, modify it by hand, review it and understand it, adapt it to the large vision of the codebase stored in your head, then that's different. In my opinion, if you follow that process you can maintain a similar claim to authorship as copying code from a book or website. However, it's now possible to skip that due to the level of copy and pasting you are able to do now. So that's how I divide up the authorship question of AI: its now possible, but not mandatory, to let an AI write something for you, but you can use AI in incremental, "lookup" ways in which I believe its okay to still claim you wrote it.

Let's turn to what people have to say about letting AI's write for you. This metaphor helped me understand why I find some pro-AI coding claims flat out ridiculous, while still reconciling that with how useful AI can be for software development.

The worst I've heard, that I can confirm to not be a joke or online trolling, is a lecture I had in my senior year, computer science capstone class. As part of this class, you are meant to learn agile, and some consultants come in to talk to you about the subject. However, in our year, one of these consultants went off on a tangent at the end, talking about how he had built their new task tracking software completely with AI and already published it to the app store. I will always remember him saying, to a class of about 80 senior computer science students, "I don't have to understand code anymore, I just release it." Yeah, the silence in the room after that was pretty loud.

Lets examine this example under the idea that AI coding, is like an extreme version of looking up coding snippets from the internet. If you built an app and released, without ever actually looking at what you were copying and pasting, what kinds of problems would you run into?

What if you wanted to add new features? Well, you could copy and paste the previous snippets back into a search engine, and hope that someone else had written code similar enough to what you had pieced together. But this has an entropic feeling: at some point, your code becomes unique enough (when viewed all together), that stackoverflow isn't going to help you anymore at that high level. This example gets even worse if you consider a system breaking bug that is very exclusive to your codebase - if you can't articulate what the problem is relating to, or where it's occurring, how is google supposed to help you?

We can also consider the security problems that arise from this approach. How do you know those pieces of code you are copying from the internet are safe in any way? Well, by reading them... its not impossible, you just have to do it. Nowadays, this problem has shown to be the bigger of the two, with packages everywhere getting pwned by AI "lookups" that authors don't bother to check. The solution is to understand what snippets you are using, even if you didn't write it yourself. There is truly no excuse for that.

AI, like google, has a lot of specific, helpful knowledge but isn't sure how it relates to the problem you have in your head. A problem that demonstrated this well, is when I used Claude to help clean up my Mac laptop that had run out of disk space. Most of it was taken up by "system data" that I had seemingly no control over. I had searched (in a search engine) on and off for months trying to figure out where this "system data" was hiding, and how I could remove it, to no avail. Mostly, I was just served ads about paid software that swore it could fix the problem.

So, I turned to asking Claude about this. It gave some example locations to try search for, but nothing was turning up. After it suggested looking in a directory for cached application data, I asked it if a cached software updated could be taking up the space. It said something along the lines of, "no, that is impossible for your situation." But after directly telling it to give me a command to show if there were cached updates, it did, and sure enough that was the problem. Then, Claude became very helpful again, helping me run the commands needed to safely remove the old update data. While AI lookups gave me lots of great help in this process, they didn't do it perfectly because just asking google "why is my computer full" didn't really work either. You had to do some thinking yourself about what the problem could be, and what the AI is giving you as a result, and keep narrowing down what you are looking for until you get a solution.

A common argument I've heard against this is that I'm not thinking "agentic" enough. If I had used the right harness, or a larger model, or just spent more tokens and let the AI truly take the wheel, all of these things could be ruled out. While models and harness are getting better every day in 2026, they are also growing more expensive. Unless a breakthrough in model efficiency happens soon, I don't want to spend $10 in tokens to find that Mac storage problem, when with a tiny bit of human thinking paired with the AI, I can do it for free (or on the free plan atleast). I think this also ties in with my environmental opinions. Datacenters existed before AI but were never a huge problem before because people cared about efficiency in their software. I care about efficiency in AI, and I think embracing that and using it scarcely and locally when I can, helps justify using it for its real benefits. 

This metaphor will extend its usefulness one last time on another common concern, by helping me explain how AI impacts learning. If you wanted to learn a calculus class, just looking up every answer to the questions obviously doesn't help you learn anything. Sure, with the AI lookup, you might think you are talking to something, which activates a different region of your brain that muddles what is really happening. But, in essence, you must be careful about what you are looking up. Looking up answers to your own questions, about how the integral is different than the Riemann sum, is a lookup that will help you learn something, but a lookup about the answer to each question will not. Like everything else in this blog, this is just opinions, but I was in college from 2022-2026, and this tracks with the lessons learned by myself and others in my year.

But if these lookups are so powerful, powerful enough that it seems like no knowledge of practical is outside of their grasp, what should people even learn? Again, keeping to the field I have experience in, there are still lots of important things to learn about software other than just the syntax or the best way to write a data structure. The best example I have for this is considering the best code reviewer you know at an organization: someone who everyone knows they can turn to make sure their code is safe and ready to deploy. Does this person seem like they are worried about not having enough work? To me, they seem like they have more work than ever. I think this kind of skill will become more important in the future, and its not a trivial one. Managing the flow and organization of information, whether it be about compute, security, safety, features, time, or cost, is a really hard problem, and one that AI in its current state isn't capable of addressing easily. The "future of work" might just be a lot more management and collecting and transiting information. You can also consider machinists: a role that, when specialized, is in high demand. Even though a machinist doesn't really do any manual machining or setup, they still have to do a lot of working managing, approving, fixing or shipping orders.

And if someday AI does become capable for doing all of those things in the future, I think the most important take away is to never give up on trying to contribute to the world. AI might "solve" software some time in the future, but as long as there is a human left on the planet, there will be problems to solve. People, including the person reading this, are flexible, and can be a lot tougher than you think. Its possible to spend years studying something that gets automated when you graduate, but that doesn't mean you didn't learn something. If you challenged yourself, not just with classes but in all aspects of your life, you learned to work hard and preserve. If you learned how to approach a problem and not give up until its solved, AI can't win against you. Atleast, not until it breaks out the nuclear warheads and phased plasma rifles in the 40 watt range.

Now, for the practical bits. Now that we have gone over how I believe AI should be used in general, how specifically should we use it?

I am an advocate for trying to use open-weight models as much as possible, but not necessarily exclusively. The reasons are this are two fold: most of the time, you don't need Ultrathink to solve the simple compilation error you are trying to fix, and secondly it helps you not form a reliance on a temporary service. Lets dive into both of these a bit more.

First, the case for not having to use the strongest model all of the time. Obviously, in the current state of hardware this is simply much cheaper, faster, and more efficient. Those are all good things, by the way, in case AI hyperscalers are asking you to think differently. So, we should try to use these models when possible, and most tasks can actually be done by today's small, dense models, in a good harness atleast. Things like removing duplicated code, checking for side effects in functions, or fixing small compile mistakes, are all easily and repeatably accomplished by something like Qwen3.6 and Pi.

2

Ok, but what open weights models to use? I personally am only able to run Qwen3.6 variants, but I think this point really isn't as important. It depends too much on hardware (and timing) to recommend an overall solution. However, I don't think this is necessarily a bad thing. It gives people a choice on how much they want to learn about AI before they use it. Casual users can download ollama, and just use whatever is recommended, but pro users inherently have to know more about the AI they are using if they want to get better information from it. Setting up kvcaches, memory offloading, compaction all have to be learned if you want to really get the most out of open-weight AI models.

In terms of actually interacting with these models, llama.cpp (and maybe ollama?) provide sleek web ui interface built in, so I just recommend them for that. For coding harness, pi is a great tool, that has a <Link href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Dli5slNaJu0">fairly level-headed team behind it</Link>, which gives me trust that it wouldn't compromise my computer. It also has the most customization I've seen in a harness tool, so bonus points there for the same reasons above. You know exactly what your agent can and cannot do on your computer, because you set it up that way.

I also think that using a coding agent is pretty useless unless you have a good handle on version control. I think using AI actually motivated me to learn more about git, and commit more of the cli commands to memory, so in that way, AI did help me learn more. Containers are also great for keeping AI's sandboxed to certain parts of your computer, and also giving them repeatable environments to start or restore from. I also expanded my knowledge in this area after using AI for coding.
